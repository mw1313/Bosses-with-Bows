# Subject Lines and Copywriting

## The Real Reason People Open Emails

The email marketing industry is obsessed with subject lines. Subject lines matter. But they're not the primary reason people open emails.

The sender name is.

When someone scans their inbox, the first thing that registers is who it's from. Recognised sender with a positive association: they open it. The subject line is a tiebreaker, not the deciding factor.

Consistency is non-negotiable. Send from the same name every time. Don't rotate between brand name, a team member's name, and a department name. Recognition is built through repetition. Disrupt it and you lose the thing that actually gets emails opened.

The from name must match what subscribers signed up for. Signed up for WidgetCo: send from WidgetCo. Not "The WidgetCo Team". Not "Sarah at WidgetCo". Not noreply@. The closer the from name matches expectations, the stronger the recognition signal.

Sporadic sending destroys sender familiarity. Every send reinforces recognition. Every gap erodes it. One of the strongest arguments for a consistent cadence.

Welcome sequences are critical here. The first few emails are when recognition and trust form. A poor welcome experience doesn't just waste the high-engagement window; it prevents the sender familiarity that drives long-term opens from developing at all.

The subject line gets them to open an email they're on the fence about. The sender name gets them to open it without thinking. Be a sender worth recognising first.

## Subject Lines

### The Five Elements

**Clarity:** Honest about what's in the email. Bait-and-switch subject lines work once and permanently damage trust. "New Product Launch: 20% Off Today Only" outperforms "Important Update".

**Relevance:** Makes the relationship feel alive. Good segmentation makes this much easier.

**Urgency:** Time-limited offers, limited stock, closing dates. Works well used sparingly. Used constantly, subscribers stop believing it.

**Curiosity:** Questions, teasers, numbers. "5 things about X" works because humans are wired to close open loops. "The secret to Y" works because secrets imply exclusive value.

**Personalisation:** Name, location, referenced past behaviour. Not always necessary but often valuable.

### Technical Considerations

- **Length:** Aim for 50 characters or fewer for reliable mobile display. Many ISP's truncate subject lines beyond this.
- **Preheader text:** The preview snippet shown after the subject line in inbox view. Treated as an afterthought by most senders; use it as a second hook.
- **Emojis:** Work in some contexts for some audiences. Test rather than assume. Don't use multiple.
- **A/B testing subject lines:** Most commonly tested element in email. But be honest about sample sizes — most subject line tests have insufficient statistical power to be reliable.

### What Doesn't Work

- Deceptive "Re:" or "Fwd:" prefixes → destroys trust
- Vague teaser subject lines that deliver nothing on opening → unsubscribes

## Email Copywriting

### Core Principles

**Know your audience.** Who are you writing to? What problem are they trying to solve? What language do they use? Build a mental model of the reader and write directly to them.

**Write for humans.** Short sentences. Active voice. Contractions. Conversational tone. "You'll save 20%" is stronger than "A saving of 20% will be made available". Read copy aloud. If you wouldn't say it, don't write it.

**One email, one job.** Every email should have a single primary purpose. One main call to action. Emails that try to do five things usually accomplish none of them.

**Structure for scanners.** Most people scan before they read. Use visual hierarchy: headlines, subheadings (if the email warrants them), bold text on key phrases. Short paragraphs. White space. The gist should be extractable in five seconds.

**The P.S.** Often the most-read part of an email after the subject line. Use it to reinforce the offer, add a bonus, or include social proof.

**Storytelling.** The most engaging emails tell a story. Customer success stories, personal anecdotes, case studies. Position your product or service as the solution to a problem your reader recognises.

### The Email Anatomy

1. **Subject line** — Gets it opened
2. **Preheader text** — Second hook in inbox view
3. **Sender name** — Builds recognition and trust; use your brand name, not "noreply"
4. **Headline/opening line** — Hooks the reader into the body
5. **Body copy** — Makes the case; builds desire or urgency; tells the story
6. **CTA** — Clear, action-oriented, visually prominent
7. **P.S.** — Final nudge; often more read than the body

### CTAs: Getting Them Right

- Specific beats generic: "Get my free template" beats "Click here"
- Buttons typically outperform text links for primary CTAs
- Make it visually distinct — it should be impossible to miss
- Repeat for longer emails: one at the top, one at the bottom
- Limit to one primary CTA; secondary CTAs should be visually subordinate

### Spam Trigger Avoidance

Content-based spam filters look for: excessive capitalisation, certain trigger words ("FREE!!!", "act now", "limited time offer" used carelessly), excessive punctuation, suspicious link-to-content ratios, broken HTML. Write like a human and most of this takes care of itself.

### Legal Requirements (Every Marketing Email)

- Physical postal address
- Working unsubscribe mechanism (honoured immediately)
- Honest subject line (not deceptive)
- Sender clearly identified
